The Laws

There are many laws when it comes to UI/UX. But we will be touch just the bare essentials here.

Miller's Law

Miller’s law teaches us to use chunking to organize content into smaller clusters to help users process, understand, and memorize easily. The longer it is, the less of it people will read.

  • Chunking Information: Break down complex information into smaller "chunks" or groups of related items. This can help users process and remember the content more effectively. For instance, when presenting a list of features, divide them into meaningful categories or groups of three to five items each.
  • Use Visual Hierarchy: Employ visual hierarchy to guide users' attention and help them understand the relationships between different pieces of content. Larger fonts, bold text, contrasting colors, and strategic placement can all help emphasize important information.
  • Progressive Disclosure: Instead of overwhelming users with a multitude of options or details all at once, consider using progressive disclosure. Start with essential information and provide options for users to delve deeper into more detailed content if they choose to.
  • Simplify Navigation: Limit the number of navigation options and categories to a manageable amount. If you have a complex website with numerous sections, consider using drop-down menus, subcategories, or search functionalities to help users find what they're looking for.
  • Clear and Concise Language: Use concise and straightforward language to convey your message. Avoid unnecessary jargon and complicated phrasing that can strain user's working memory.
  • Visual Aids: Incorporate visual aids such as icons, images, and diagrams to assist in conveying information more efficiently. Visual elements can enhance comprehension and reduce the cognitive load on users.

Fitts' Law

The time to acquire a target is a function of the distance to and size of the target.

  • Touch targets should be large enough for users to accurately select them. - WCAG recommends minimum of 44 × 44 CSS px.
  • Touch targets should have ample spacing between them. - google's material design recommends touch targets should be separated by 8 dp minimum.
  • Touch targets should be placed in areas of an interface that allow them to be easily acquired - turns out people prefer to touch the middle of the screen more than the edges.

Jacob's Law

Users spend most of their time on other sites, and they prefer your site to work the same way as all the other sites they already know.

  • Users will transfer expectations they have built around one familiar product to another that appears similar.
  • By leveraging existing mental models, we can create superior user experiences in which the users can focus on their tasks rather than on learning new models.
  • When making changes, minimize discomfort by empowering users to continue using a familiar version for a limited time.

Doherty Threshold

Productivity soars when a computer and its users interact at a pace ( <400 ms) that ensures that neither has to wait on the other.

  • Provide system feedback within 400 ms in order to keep users’ attention and increase productivity.
  • Use perceived performance to improve response time and reduce the perception of waiting.
  • Animation is one way to visually engage people while loading or processing is happening in the background.
  • Progress bars help make wait times tolerable, regardless of their accuracy.
  • Purposefully adding a delay to a process can actually increase its perceived value and instill a sense of trust, even when the process itself actually takes much less time.

Law of Proximity/ Similarity/ Common Region/ Continuity

Place elements that belong together closer to one another. Similar elements are perceived as belonging together. Use similar color, spacing and size for such elements. Try to put to well defined border around the similar elements.

Law of Closure

Refers to the human tendency to perceive incomplete or fragmented visual elements as complete objects. In terms of web design, the law of closure suggests that users will mentally fill in the gaps and complete shapes or patterns that are not fully defined. This principle can be applied to create visually pleasing and intuitive web interfaces.

Law of Figure and Ground

Visuals are either a figure or as a background element. Design pages with clear foreground and background, this helps users decide what to focus on.

Law of symmetry

Humans prefer symmetry and that's why they call you ugly. Symmetry creates an impression of stability and order and can also support a clear structure of content.

Accessibility

The internet is a crucial part of modern life, providing access to information, services, education, entertainment, and more. Ensuring that websites are accessible allows people with disabilities to participate fully in online activities, promoting equal access and inclusivity. The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) provide a globally recognized framework for creating accessible web content.

Color Contrast

The World Wide Web Consortium (2016) recommends a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 to the background for small texts and a contrast ratio of at least 3:1 for large texts (from 14 pt bold /18 pt normal print). The contrast can be checked, for example, on the website contrastchecker.com

Appropriate Line Spacing and Letter-Spacing

The appropriate line spacing (also known as line height) and letter spacing (also known as tracking) for a website's text can have a significant impact on its readability and accessibility.

Line Spacing (Line Height)

Lines that are excessively long can be challenging to read because readers struggle to trace back to the start of the next line. Conversely, overly short lines can create a disjointed reading experience, prompting readers to skip back and forth frequently. The ideal line length typically falls between 50 to 75 characters per line, including spaces. This range is considered optimal for most reading scenarios, as it allows readers to easily follow the text without losing their pace.

  • Avoid Crowded Text: Line spacing should be generous enough to prevent text from feeling crowded or cramped. This makes it easier for users to distinguish between lines of text.
  • Balanced Spacing: Aim for a balance between too much and too little line spacing. Too much spacing can make it challenging for users to connect lines of text, while too little spacing can cause lines to blend together.
  • Readability: Ensure that lines of text do not overlap or touch each other, as this can impede readability.
  • Consistency: Maintain consistent line spacing throughout your website for a harmonious reading experience. In general, a line spacing of 1.4 to 1.6 times the font size is a common guideline to follow.

Letter Spacing (Tracking)

  • Avoid Excessive Tracking: Letter spacing that is too wide can make text difficult to read, as words lose their cohesion and become disjointed.
  • Natural and Readable: Aim for a letter spacing that maintains the natural flow of words while providing a little extra room between letters for clarity.
  • Font Consideration: Some fonts inherently require more or less letter spacing due to their design. Choose fonts that are designed with readability in mind, and adjust letter spacing accordingly.
  • Consistency: As with line spacing, maintain a consistent letter spacing across your website's text to create a cohesive visual experience.

Minimum Font size

Text should have a minimum font size of 16 pixels (roughly 12 points) for body text. This ensures that most users,including those with mild visual impairments, can read the text without needing to zoom in.
Use relative units like percentages (%) or "em" units instead of fixed pixel values for font sizes. This enables the font to adjust proportionally to the user's preferred text size settings.

Breakpoints

Breakpoints in web design refer to specific screen widths at which the layout of a website or web application adjusts to provide an optimal user experience. These breakpoints are crucial for creating responsive designs that adapt to various devices, screen sizes, and orientations. By defining breakpoints, you can ensure that your content remains readable and usable across a wide range of devices, from smartphones to large desktop monitors. They are used to apply rules to elements such as Font Sizes, Navigation, Cropped or dynamically-resized Images & Videos. Breakpoints are typically defined using media queries in CSS.

/* extra small screens (up to 576px) */
@media (max-width: 576px) { /* CSS rules for Extra Small screens */ }
/* small screens (577px to 768px) */
@media (min-width: 577px) and (max-width: 768px) { /* CSS rules for Small screens */ }
/* medium screens (769px to 992px) */
@media (min-width: 769px) and (max-width: 992px) { /* CSS rules for Medium screens */ }
/* Large screens (993px to 1200px) */
@media (min-width: 993px) and (max-width: 1200px) { /* CSS rules for large screens */ }
/* Extra Large screens (1201px and above) */
@media (max-width: 1201px) { /* CSS rules for Extra Large screens */ }

Accordions - hide supplementary content

Accordions are a common UI pattern used in web design to hide and reveal content in a collapsible manner. They're particularly useful when you have content that might be less important or supplementary, allowing users to focus on what's essential while still providing access to additional information.

Commonly used for FAQs, Product Details and Descriptions, Tutorials and Documentation, User Profiles and Dashboards, Mobile Navigation, Privacy Policies and Terms of Service, Filters and Search Options.

  • Use Clear Titles Provide clear and concise titles for each accordion section. The title should give users a hint about the content within, helping them make informed decisions about whether to expand it.
  • Mobile-Friendly Design Accordions are especially useful on mobile devices where screen space is limited. They allow users to easily expand and collapse content sections without overwhelming the screen.
  • Animation and Indicators Include a subtle animation when opening or closing an accordion section. Also, use visual indicators like arrows or "+" and "-" signs to show which sections can be expanded or collapsed.
  • Avoid Overuse While accordions can help manage content, be cautious not to overuse them. If there's only a small amount of content in an accordion, it might be better to display it without collapsing it.
  • Maintain Accessibility Ensure that the accordion interactions are accessible to all users, including those who use screen readers or keyboard navigation. Use appropriate ARIA attributes to convey the state of the accordion to assistive technologies.
  • Progressive Disclosure Consider the concept of progressive disclosure, where you present information in layers. Users can access more detailed information as they choose, which helps prevent overwhelming them with too much information at once.

Color

The colors used on a website should harmonize well with each other. Use Adobe Color wheel, and realtimecolors.

60-30-10 rule

Adhere to the 60-30-10 guideline for direction. As per this guideline, a layout should encompass 60% neutral color (such as the background), 30% contrasting color (used for headers, for example), and 10% accent color (employed in elements like call-to-action buttons). This division not only establishes a visual hierarchy but also fosters a harmonious color interplay.

Harmonious Gradients

For optimal results, stick to monochromatic or analogous colors for color transitions. When dealing with complementary colors, seamless color progressions devoid of grayish patches can be accomplished by introducing a hue that sits between the complementary colors on the color wheel.

Symmetrical Balance

balance is achieved by placing elements equally on either side of a central axis.

Asymmetrical Balance

involves skillfully arranging objects varying in size, color, or shape to achieve equilibrium in visual weight, even with their differences. For instance, a sizable yet empty object can be counterbalanced by a smaller, but denser, filled object. This type of balance imbues designs with a heightened sense of dynamism and intrigue, evoking feelings of motion and vitality, which often surpass the static nature of symmetrical balance.

Radial

Generating radial balance requires the establishment of a focal point, around which the remaining elements find equilibrium. This can be accomplished by arranging objects, colors, or textures at equidistant intervals from the center, or by orchestrating a spiral pattern that either converges toward or diverges from the central point. As the focal point garners the most attention, web designers frequently designate it for pivotal components like CTAs or other essential clickable elements.
Notably, radial balance finds its application in pricing pages, effectively spotlighting a specific pricing model. Designers commonly employ variations in size or color to emphasize the central point. This approach echoes Hick's law, asserting that the time spent making a decision is directly linked to the number of available choices.
